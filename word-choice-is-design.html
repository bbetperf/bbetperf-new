How Apple Maps Guides You, Not Just Routes You.

Word Choice Is Design. Mar 26.

A reflection on how Apple Maps uses voice intonation to manage your emotional state while driving, and why Google Maps and Waze don't.

Not Imagination.

The first time I noticed it, I thought I was imagining things. Apple Maps said "turn left onto Main Street" and something felt different. Not the voice quality, not the volume. The phrasing. "Turn left onto" instead of just "turn left in 300 meters." A preposition. A street name. A sentence that sounded like something a person would actually say.

So I started paying attention. Apple Maps consistently uses phrasing that sounds like conversation. "Bear right toward the highway." "Keep left to stay on this road." "Your destination is on the right." These aren't robotic instructions. They're miniature sentences with context baked in. They tell you not just what to do, but why, and where you're going, and what to expect.

What Everyone Else Does.

Google Maps says "turn left." Waze says "turn left." These are commands. They're efficient, but they have no texture. They assume you don't need context. They treat the driver like a machine receiving input. Apple Maps makes you feel like someone is in the passenger seat, thinking alongside you. And that feeling reduces stress, especially in unfamiliar cities or complex interchanges. The emotional result of the word choice is a design decision. And most people designing navigation tools have never thought about it that way.
