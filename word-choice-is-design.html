Word Choice Is Design.

Reading. Mar 26.

A reflection on how Apple Maps uses voice intonation to manage your emotional state while driving, and why Google Maps and Waze don't.

The Observation.

Apple Maps says "Turn left in 500 feet." Google Maps says "In 500 feet, turn left." Waze says "Turn left. Then, in 300 feet, turn right." Three different apps, three different ways of constructing the same sentence. The difference isn't grammatical — it's emotional.

Apple leads with the action. The instruction lands first, the distance second. This isn't an accident. It mirrors how a calm, confident co-pilot actually speaks. You hear what you need to do before you hear how long you have. Your hands start moving before your brain finishes parsing the sentence.

The Implication.

Every word in a product is a design decision. Not just the button labels and error messages — the word order, the sentence structure, the choice of "tap" versus "press" versus "select." These choices accumulate into a voice that either creates anxiety or reduces it.

Navigation apps are an extreme case because the stakes are physical — a missed turn, a distraction at 60mph. But the same principle applies to every interface that asks something of you under any kind of pressure. Word choice is not copywriting. It's interaction design.
